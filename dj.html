04 — DJ

On Being A DJ

The most fascinating thing to me at a house party during my college years was the bright lights, colors and buttons of my friend's DJ deck. I took to DJing quickly. What began as lessons in how to operate the board — from a friend who knew how to DJ — became back-to-back sessions lasting hours. Realizing how much fun I was having, I reached out to a venue to see if they would be willing to have me play.

The Story

My first gig was in the basement of Fenway Johnnies, now closed, in Boston. The night ended early due to maintenance issues, but it hooked me on the experience. Over a few weeks, I made my way upstairs. Moving up — literally and figuratively — other DJs started noticing my sets. I initiated discussions with other bars and began playing across Boston. Networking was crucial to my success: I had to connect with other DJs and management to get gigs. Under a year into my career, Big Night Event Group released a contest for a chance to open at the biggest venue in Boston. With pushes from a DJ friend, I submitted a mix. While I didn't win, I did get the attention of the event group — and shortly thereafter I became one of their resident DJs, playing huge clubs for big names.

The Lessons

While at Big Night Event Group, DJing became significantly harder. It became less about the transitions of the music — a defined skill shared across all of their DJs — and more about the curation of the night. I was an opener at these clubs, with the primary responsibility of getting the crowd excited for the headliner. Each venue and headliner has a different energy, and each set was as unique as the crowd in front of it. The first hour was always about reading the room and seeing what music stuck. Every night was different. I learned a lot about reading a crowd, thinking quickly, and how to change course when things aren't right. By the end of every night, I got everybody's hands up.
